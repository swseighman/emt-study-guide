## EMT Study Guide: Common Medical  & Trauma Conditions

### Respiratory Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Asthma Attack** | • Wheezing<br>• Dyspnea (shortness of breath)<br>• Accessory muscle use<br>• Tripod positioning<br>• Anxiety<br>• Decreased oxygen saturation<br>• Inability to speak in full sentences | • Position of comfort (typically sitting upright)<br>• Administer oxygen to maintain SpO2 >94%<br>• Assist patient with prescribed inhaler (albuterol) if available<br>• Consider assisting with prescribed epinephrine auto-injector for severe cases<br>• Monitor vital signs continuously<br>• Request ALS backup for severe cases unresponsive to initial treatment<br>• Rapid transport for patients with cyanosis, altered mental status, or extreme fatigue |
| **COPD Exacerbation** | • Increased dyspnea<br>• Increased work of breathing<br>• Wheezing and rhonchi<br>• Cough with increased sputum<br>• Barrel chest<br>• Pursed-lip breathing<br>• Cyanosis<br>• Use of accessory muscles | • Position of comfort (usually semi-Fowler's or sitting)<br>• Administer oxygen cautiously (target SpO2 88-92%, not 100%)<br>• Assist with prescribed inhalers if available<br>• Monitor for respiratory depression with high-flow oxygen<br>• Consider CPAP if authorized and trained<br>• Monitor vital signs and oxygen saturation<br>• Request ALS backup for severe respiratory distress<br>• Prompt transport |
| **Pneumothorax** | • Sudden sharp chest pain<br>• Dyspnea<br>• Decreased or absent breath sounds on affected side<br>• Hypoxia<br>• Tachycardia<br>• Anxiety and restlessness<br>• Possible subcutaneous emphysema | • Administer 100% oxygen via non-rebreather mask<br>• Avoid positive pressure ventilation if possible (may expand pneumothorax)<br>• Position patient for comfort, usually semi-sitting<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Monitor for signs of tension pneumothorax<br>• Support ventilations only if necessary with BVM<br>• Continuous monitoring of vital signs and cardiac rhythm if equipped |
| **Tension Pneumothorax** | • Progressive respiratory distress<br>• Decreased breath sounds on affected side<br>• Hypotension<br>• Distended neck veins (JVD)<br>• Tracheal deviation away from affected side<br>• Cyanosis<br>• Rapid deterioration | • Immediate recognition as true emergency<br>• High-flow oxygen<br>• Rapid transport in position of comfort<br>• Request immediate ALS intercept<br>• Support ventilations as needed<br>• Monitor for cardiac arrest<br>• *Note: Needle decompression is needed but beyond EMT scope; rapid transport is critical* |
| **Pulmonary Edema** | • Dyspnea, especially when lying flat<br>• Frothy, pink-tinged sputum<br>• Crackles/rales in lungs<br>• Peripheral edema<br>• Tachycardia<br>• Tachypnea<br>• Cough<br>• Use of accessory muscles | • Position upright with legs dependent if BP allows<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Consider CPAP if authorized and available<br>• Monitor vital signs closely<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Reassessment of respiratory status frequently<br>• Be prepared for possible respiratory failure |

### Cardiac Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)** | • Chest pain/pressure/tightness<br>• Pain radiating to jaw, arm, or back<br>• Dyspnea<br>• Nausea/vomiting<br>• Diaphoresis<br>• Anxiety<br>• Fatigue<br>• Women may present with atypical symptoms | • Position of comfort (typically semi-sitting)<br>• Administer oxygen if SpO2 <94%<br>• Assist with prescribed nitroglycerin if available and BP >100 systolic<br>• Assist with prescribed aspirin (160-325mg) if no contraindications<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Continuous monitoring of vital signs<br>• Prepare for CPR and AED if condition deteriorates<br>• Rapid transport to cardiac-capable facility<br>• Document time of symptom onset |
| **Cardiac Arrest** | • Unresponsiveness<br>• Absence of breathing or abnormal breathing<br>• No pulse<br>• Cyanosis | • Begin high-quality CPR immediately (30:2 ratio if not intubated)<br>• Apply AED as soon as available and follow prompts<br>• Ensure adequate chest compressions (rate 100-120/min, depth 2-2.4 inches)<br>• Minimize interruptions in compressions<br>• Establish airway and provide ventilation<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Consider reversible causes (H's and T's)<br>• If ROSC achieved, provide oxygen and monitor closely<br>• Follow local protocols for termination or transport | 
| **Congestive Heart Failure** | • Progressive dyspnea<br>• Orthopnea<br>• Peripheral edema<br>• Fatigue<br>• Cough (possibly with frothy sputum)<br>• JVD<br>• S3 heart sound (if trained to auscultate)<br>• Crackles in lungs | • Position upright with legs dependent<br>• Administer oxygen to maintain SpO2 >94%<br>• Consider CPAP if available and authorized<br>• Monitor vital signs and respiratory status<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport to appropriate facility<br>• Reassess frequently<br>• Be prepared for respiratory deterioration |

### Neurological Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Stroke/CVA** | • Sudden onset of symptoms<br>• Facial droop<br>• Arm drift or weakness<br>• Speech difficulties<br>• Vision changes<br>• Severe headache<br>• Altered mental status<br>• Balance difficulties<br>• Numbness/tingling | • Perform stroke scale assessment (Cincinnati or similar)<br>• Establish time of symptom onset (critical for treatment options)<br>• Maintain airway and administer oxygen if needed<br>• Position with head elevated 15-30° if no trauma<br>• Protect affected limbs<br>• Monitor vital signs closely<br>• Check blood glucose level<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to stroke center<br>• Alert receiving facility early (stroke team activation)<br>• Reassess neurological status frequently |
| **Seizure** | • Loss of consciousness<br>• Muscle rigidity followed by rhythmic jerking<br>• Possible incontinence<br>• Post-ictal confusion<br>• Possible tongue biting<br>• Altered mental status | • Protect patient from injury during seizure<br>• Time the seizure<br>• Do not restrain or place anything in mouth<br>• Position on side after seizure (recovery position)<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Check blood glucose if equipped<br>• Request ALS for status epilepticus (seizures >5 min or recurrent)<br>• Monitor airway and breathing<br>• Transport if first-time seizure, status epilepticus, or injured during seizure<br>• Document seizure characteristics and duration |
| **Status Epilepticus** | • Continuous seizure lasting >5 minutes<br>• Multiple seizures without regaining consciousness<br>• Progressive hypoxia<br>• Hyperthermia<br>• Injury from prolonged muscle activity | • Request ALS immediately<br>• Ensure patent airway<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Check blood glucose if equipped<br>• Protect from further injury<br>• Consider nasopharyngeal airway if prolonged<br>• Monitor vital signs<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Be prepared for respiratory compromise<br>• Consider positioning to prevent aspiration |

### Trauma Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Traumatic Brain Injury** | • Altered level of consciousness<br>• Unequal pupils<br>• Raccoon eyes or Battle's sign (late)<br>• CSF from ears or nose<br>• Vomiting<br>• Seizures<br>• Amnesia<br>• Abnormal posturing<br>• Headache<br>• Combativeness | • Maintain cervical spine stabilization<br>• Ensure patent airway with jaw thrust if needed<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Prevent hypoxia (maintain SpO2 >94%)<br>• Elevation of head 15-30° unless contraindicated<br>• Monitor vital signs frequently (watch for Cushing's triad)<br>• Check pupils for changes<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to trauma center<br>• Monitor for vomiting and seizures<br>• Reassess GCS frequently |
| **Spinal Cord Injury** | • Pain along spine<br>• Paralysis or weakness<br>• Loss of sensation<br>• Priapism<br>• Loss of bowel/bladder control<br>• Diaphragmatic breathing<br>• Neurogenic shock (hypotension with bradycardia)<br>• Sensation level | • Manual cervical spine stabilization immediately<br>• Apply cervical collar<br>• Log roll for assessment and positioning on backboard<br>• Secure patient to backboard with straps and head blocks<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Maintain normal body temperature (prevent hypothermia)<br>• Monitor respiratory status closely (diaphragm may be affected)<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to trauma center<br>• Reassess neurological function frequently |
| **Flail Chest** | • Paradoxical chest wall movement<br>• Severe pain with breathing<br>• Dyspnea<br>• Tachypnea<br>• Cyanosis<br>• Decreased breath sounds<br>• Subcutaneous emphysema | • Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Position of comfort unless contraindicated<br>• Consider stabilizing flail segment with hand or bulky dressing<br>• Monitor for tension pneumothorax<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Consider assisted ventilation if respiratory failure develops<br>• Monitor SpO2 continuously<br>• Reassess breath sounds frequently |
| **Hemorrhagic Shock** | • Tachycardia<br>• Hypotension<br>• Pale, cool, clammy skin<br>• Delayed capillary refill<br>• Anxiety progressing to lethargy<br>• Thirst<br>• Decreased urine output<br>• Altered mental status | • Control external bleeding with direct pressure<br>• Apply tourniquets for severe extremity hemorrhage<br>• Apply pelvic binder for suspected pelvic fracture<br>• Position supine with legs elevated if no contraindications<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Keep patient warm (prevent hypothermia)<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to trauma center<br>• Reassess vital signs frequently<br>• Consider permissive hypotension (avoid excessive fluid resuscitation) |
| **Open Pneumothorax** | • Open chest wound<br>• Sucking sound with breathing<br>• Dyspnea<br>• Decreased breath sounds on affected side<br>• Subcutaneous emphysema<br>• Cyanosis<br>• Tachycardia | • Apply three-sided occlusive dressing immediately<br>• Seal on three sides only (allow air to escape)<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Position patient on affected side if tolerated<br>• Monitor for tension pneumothorax<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to trauma center<br>• Be prepared to remove dressing if tension develops<br>• Monitor respiratory status continuously |
| **Abdominal Evisceration** | • Exposed abdominal contents<br>• Severe pain<br>• Nausea/vomiting<br>• Signs of shock<br>• Guarding/rigidity of abdomen | • Do not attempt to replace organs into abdomen<br>• Cover exposed organs with sterile, moist saline dressings<br>• Secure with dry dressings over moist dressings<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Position supine with knees slightly flexed<br>• Keep patient warm<br>• Monitor for signs of shock<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Rapid transport to trauma center<br>• Frequent reassessment of vital signs |
| **Extremity Fractures** | • Pain and tenderness<br>• Swelling<br>• Deformity<br>• Crepitus<br>• Limited movement<br>• Bruising<br>• Open wound at fracture site (if open)<br>• Possible neurovascular compromise | • Control bleeding if present<br>• Assess distal pulses, motor, and sensation<br>• Immobilize joint above and below fracture<br>• Splint in position found if neurovascular function intact<br>• Gentle alignment if neurovascular compromise present<br>• Elevate extremity if possible<br>• Apply cold pack<br>• Reassess neurovascular status after splinting<br>• Consider pain management options within scope<br>• Transport to appropriate facility |

### Medical Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Diabetic Emergencies - Hypoglycemia** | • Altered mental status<br>• Diaphoresis<br>• Tachycardia<br>• Hunger<br>• Weakness<br>• Seizures<br>• Confusion<br>• Combativeness | • Assess blood glucose if equipped<br>• If conscious and able to swallow: administer oral glucose<br>• If unconscious: recovery position and request ALS<br>• Protect airway<br>• Administer oxygen if indicated<br>• Transport to appropriate facility<br>• Reassess frequently<br>• If patient improves with oral glucose, encourage complex carbohydrate intake<br>• Document response to treatment |
| **Diabetic Emergencies - Hyperglycemia (DKA)** | • Gradual onset<br>• Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia<br>• Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid)<br>• Fruity breath odor<br>• Dehydration<br>• Weakness<br>• Altered mental status<br>• Nausea/vomiting | • Administer oxygen if indicated<br>• Check blood glucose if equipped<br>• Maintain airway<br>• Position for comfort and airway protection<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport to appropriate facility<br>• Monitor vital signs closely<br>• Consider small amounts of water if patient alert and requesting fluids<br>• Be prepared for deterioration in mental status |
| **Anaphylaxis** | • Sudden onset<br>• Hives/urticaria<br>• Angioedema<br>• Respiratory distress/stridor<br>• Hypotension<br>• Tachycardia<br>• GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)<br>• Sense of impending doom | • Remove allergen if possible<br>• Position of comfort (usually sitting upright)<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Assist with patient's epinephrine auto-injector if available<br>• Consider administering epinephrine via auto-injector per protocol<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Monitor airway continuously<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Be prepared for airway compromise and cardiac arrest<br>• Reassess frequently |
| **Heat Exhaustion** | • Heavy sweating<br>• Weakness, fatigue<br>• Headache<br>• Nausea/vomiting<br>• Muscle cramps<br>• Normal or slightly elevated temperature<br>• Dizziness<br>• Pale, moist skin | • Move to cool environment<br>• Remove excess clothing<br>• Apply cool, wet towels<br>• Fan patient<br>• Oral rehydration if alert (water or electrolyte solution)<br>• Monitor vital signs<br>• Transport if symptoms persist<br>• Monitor for progression to heat stroke<br>• Document temperature if equipment available |
| **Heat Stroke** | • Hot, dry skin (classic) or sweating (exertional)<br>• Very high body temperature (>104°F/40°C)<br>• Altered mental status<br>• Tachycardia<br>• Hypotension<br>• Seizures<br>• Possible loss of consciousness | • Move to cool environment immediately<br>• Remove clothing<br>• Active cooling (ice packs to axilla, groin, neck)<br>• Wet patient and fan vigorously<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Monitor core temperature if equipped<br>• Be prepared for seizures and cardiac arrest<br>• Continuous monitoring of vital signs |
| **Hypothermia** | • Shivering (early) or absence of shivering (late)<br>• Altered mental status<br>• Pale, cold skin<br>• Bradycardia<br>• Hypotension<br>• Decreased respiratory rate<br>• Stiff or rigid posture<br>• Possible frostbite | • Remove wet clothing<br>• Prevent further heat loss (insulate from ground)<br>• Apply warm blankets<br>• Handle gently (risk of ventricular fibrillation)<br>• Administer warm, humidified oxygen if available<br>• Active rewarming of truncal areas only<br>• No food or drink if altered mental status<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport in warm ambulance<br>• Check core temperature if equipped<br>• If cardiac arrest: prolonged resuscitation efforts |
| **Opioid Overdose** | • Respiratory depression<br>• Pinpoint pupils<br>• Decreased level of consciousness<br>• Hypotension<br>• Bradycardia<br>• Cyanosis<br>• Evidence of drug use | • Ensure scene safety<br>• Assess airway and breathing<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Assist ventilations if inadequate breathing<br>• Administer naloxone if authorized and indicated<br>• Recovery position if breathing adequate<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport even if patient improves with naloxone<br>• Be prepared for agitation after naloxone<br>• Monitor for recurrent respiratory depression |

### Obstetric Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Normal Childbirth** | • Regular contractions<br>• Urge to push or have bowel movement<br>• Crowning<br>• Rupture of membranes<br>• Bloody show | • Position mother on firm surface with knees flexed<br>• Prepare OB kit<br>• Support baby's head during delivery (prevent explosive delivery)<br>• Check for cord around neck<br>• Suction mouth then nose after head delivers<br>• Support body as it delivers<br>• Note time of birth<br>• Dry and warm baby immediately<br>• Assess APGAR at 1 and 5 minutes<br>• Clamp and cut cord after pulsations stop<br>• Deliver placenta (do not pull on cord)<br>• Massage mother's lower abdomen after placenta delivers<br>• Transport mother and baby together |
| **Postpartum Hemorrhage** | • Excessive bleeding after delivery (>500 mL)<br>• Saturated pads in <15 minutes<br>• Signs of shock<br>• Boggy uterus | • Massage uterine fundus (lower abdomen)<br>• Allow baby to nurse if possible<br>• Apply external bimanual compression if trained<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Position flat with legs elevated<br>• Collect all blood-soaked materials for estimation<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Rapid transport<br>• Save placenta for inspection at hospital<br>• Monitor vital signs continuously |
| **Prolapsed Umbilical Cord** | • Visible cord preceding fetal presenting part<br>• Cord pulsation may be present or absent | • Position mother in knee-chest position or Trendelenburg<br>• Insert gloved hand into vagina to push presenting part off cord<br>• Cover exposed cord with moist, sterile dressing<br>• Administer oxygen to mother<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Emergency transport<br>• Maintain manual displacement until arrival at hospital<br>• Do not attempt to replace cord<br>• Monitor cord for pulsations if possible |

### Pediatric Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Respiratory Distress - Croup** | • Barking cough<br>• Inspiratory stridor<br>• Low-grade fever<br>• Worse at night<br>• Hoarse voice<br>• Mild to moderate distress | • Keep child calm (parental presence helps)<br>• Position of comfort (usually upright in parent's lap)<br>• Cool mist if available<br>• Administer oxygen if tolerated<br>• Monitor respiratory status<br>• Transport in position of comfort<br>• Consider ALS for moderate to severe distress<br>• Reassess frequently |
| **Respiratory Distress - Epiglottitis** | • Drooling<br>• High fever<br>• Tripod positioning<br>• Muffled voice<br>• Stridor<br>• Rapid deterioration possible<br>• "Toxically ill" appearance | • Allow position of comfort (typically sitting forward)<br>• Keep child calm<br>• Do NOT examine throat or attempt to visualize epiglottis<br>• Administer cool oxygen if tolerated<br>• Request ALS backup immediately<br>• Emergency transport<br>• Be prepared for rapid airway compromise<br>• Have BVM and airway equipment ready<br>• Consider allowing parent to hold oxygen near child's face |
| **Respiratory Distress - Bronchiolitis** | • Rhinorrhea<br>• Wheezing<br>• Crackles<br>• Tachypnea<br>• Retractions<br>• Low-grade fever<br>• Poor feeding | • Position of comfort<br>• Administer oxygen to maintain SpO2 >94%<br>• Nasal suctioning if needed<br>• Monitor respiratory status<br>• Transport to appropriate facility<br>• Keep child with caregiver if possible<br>• Consider ALS for moderate to severe distress<br>• Monitor for signs of respiratory failure |
| **Febrile Seizure** | • Seizure activity with fever<br>• Age 6 months to 5 years<br>• Post-ictal period<br>• History of rapid temperature rise | • Protect from injury during seizure<br>• Position on side after seizure stops<br>• Passive cooling (remove excess clothing)<br>• Do not actively cool with alcohol or ice baths<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Transport if first seizure, prolonged >5 min, or multiple seizures<br>• Reassurance to parents<br>• Document duration of seizure<br>• Monitor airway and breathing |

### Environmental Emergencies

| Condition | Signs/Symptoms | EMT Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Drowning** | • Respiratory distress/arrest<br>• Coughing/vomiting<br>• Altered mental status<br>• Cyanosis or pale skin<br>• Hypothermia | • Ensure scene safety<br>• Establish airway and breathing<br>• Administer high-flow oxygen<br>• Consider spinal immobilization if diving or trauma suspected<br>• Remove wet clothing<br>• Prevent heat loss<br>• Begin CPR if needed<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport all submersion patients even if asymptomatic<br>• Monitor for delayed pulmonary edema |
| **Lightning Strike** | • Burns (often feathering pattern)<br>• Cardiac arrest<br>• Neurological deficits<br>• Fixed/dilated pupils<br>• Tympanic membrane rupture<br>• Temporary paralysis | • Ensure scene safety<br>• Assess for multiple victims<br>• Prioritize apneic/pulseless patients (reverse triage)<br>• Begin CPR if needed<br>• Spinal immobilization if fall or blast injury<br>• Treat burns (typically superficial)<br>• Administer oxygen<br>• Request ALS backup<br>• Transport all lightning strike patients<br>• Monitor cardiac rhythm if equipped |


*Note: This study guide is based on standard EMT practice in the United States per Prehospital Emergency Care, 12th Edition. Always follow your local protocols and medical direction, as treatments may vary by region.*
